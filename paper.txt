A B S T R A C T
Residential energy poverty is the lack of accessible, reliable, clean energy services. Researchers have not reached
a consensus about defining and measuring energy poverty, leading to ineffective energy-related policy formulation.
Existing approaches simplify energy poverty to a "yes-no" approach, neglecting its multidimensional
nature. The subjective parameters and thresholds used further introduce uncertainty and bias. We addressed this
gap by formulating a comprehensive Residential Energy Poverty Index (REPI) for Indian states by adopting a
multidimensional approach, including clean and green energy, energy efficiency, accessibility and reliability,
and appliances. The current approach focuses on energy availability beyond basic needs and should look at the
broader dimensions of quality of life, socioeconomic well-being, education, and environmental sustainability.
The work is based on the Indian residential energy survey conducted by CEEW, which included 14,850 households
across 21 Indian states. REPI was constructed by assigning weightage to different variables across four
dimensions using Principal Component Analysis. Multiple Linear Regression was utilized to identify the socioeconomic
factors impacting REPI.
Findings reveal that enhancing energy efficiency and decentralized solar power, particularly in states like
Chhattisgarh and Jharkhand, can significantly improve energy access and reliability. Factors such as primary
education and female-headed households correlate with lower energy poverty, while poor housing, rural areas,
marginal groups, and primary economic activities like agriculture correlate with higher energy poverty. The
findings highlight the importance of tailored state-specific policies like decentralized renewable energy, energy
cleanliness and awareness campaigns to address residential energy poverty in India.
1. Introduction
India is the world’s third-largest primary energy consumer (IEA,
2021). Lack of access to clean and intermittent energy supply significantly
impacts millions of people’s daily lives, hindering development
and worsening socioeconomic inequalities. This situation increases the
dependency on inefficient and hazardous energy alternatives such as
kerosene lamps, dung cakes, and agricultural waste for cooking and
heating. These energy alternatives contribute to indoor air pollution and
cause respiratory illness, especially among women and children who
spend most of their time using traditional cooking stoves (Sadath and
Acharya, 2017). In 2019, household air pollution led to 6.1 lakh deaths
in India (Pandey et al., 2021).
To address the problem of indoor air pollution, the central government
launched the PM Ujjwala scheme in 2016, adding 95.9 million new
LPG connections as of July 2023 (PIB, 2023a). Similarly, the Pradhan
Mantri Sahaj Bijli Har Ghar Yojana (Saubhagya) scheme has helped 28.6
million households get electricity connections since 2017 (PIB, 2023b).
While these programs have improved energy accessibility, 40 million
people still lack stable electricity and rely on traditional biomass fuels
for cooking and heating (IEA, 2021). This burden falls disproportionately
on women, who collect biomass for cooking, limiting their
educational opportunities and participation in economic activities
(Barnes and Sen, 2004). Additionally, unsustainable harvesting of firewood
and crop residues puts pressure on fragile ecosystems, leading to
deforestation, environmental degradation and climate change. This poor
accessibility and unreliability of electricity supply and use of traditional
cooking sources result in energy poverty at the household level. Understanding
energy poverty at the household level is essential for
identifying and overcoming barriers to equitable energy access, which is
crucial for improving living standards, reducing socioeconomic
inequality, and encouraging sustainable economic growth.

contrasting levels of energy poverty, which require targeted, state-

2. Literature review
Efforts have been made to define and construct a residential energy
poverty index in literature (Bekele et al., 2015; Gupta et al., 2020;
Khandker et al., 2010; Nayan Yadava and Sinha, 2019; Nussbaumer
et al., 2012; Okushima, 2016; Pachauri et al., 2004; Thomson et al.,
2017), still there is a lack of common consensus on what defines energy
poverty and how it is measured. There are different ways in which energy
poverty is defined and measured by various studies, which are
mentioned as follows.
Residential energy poverty is frequently associated with access to
energy services for cooking and lighting (Brew-Hammond, 2010; Nayan
Yadava and Sinha, 2019; Winkler et al., 2011). Modern and clean energy
sources, such as liquified petroleum gas (LPG) and traditional fuels like
firewood and dung cakes, are considered energy sources for cooking
within the household (Brew-Hammond, 2010; Nayan Yadava and Sinha,
2019), and grid connection for the provision of electricity for lighting is
considered as energy source within the household (Winkler et al., 2011).
This index is binary, i.e., whether or not one has access and, thereby,
does not clarify their usage pattern as few households prefer using
cookstoves despite the availability of LPG. Second, it also lacks the
incorporation of crucial factors such as frequency of power outages,
low-voltage situations, uninterrupted supply, use of kerosene lamps, and
frequency of LPG refills.
Pachauri et al. (2004) measured energy poverty by considering the
energy required to meet fundamental needs. The final energy
consumption-based energy poverty index has also been used in earlier
studies (Goldemberg, 1990; Krugmann and Goldemberg, 1983). This
approach also focuses on only the availability of energy services and
does not consider crucial factors such as energy accessibility, reliability,
and usage patterns. Second, it yields variable results across urban and
rural areas, income groups, and individuals. These variations lead to
challenges in establishing a uniform and comprehensive assessment of
energy poverty.
Energy poverty is also being assessed using the expenditure
approach, which involves calculating a ratio of household income to
energy expenditure (Leach, 1987; Okushima, 2016; Thomson et al.,
2017) or creating a threshold of minimum spend on energy services for
decent living (Barnes et al., 2011). This method helps evaluate the
affordability of energy for households and provides insights into their
energy-related financial burden. Focusing solely on affordability overlooks
the broader issue of physical access to energy services, leading to
an incomplete understanding of household energy poverty. Also, as
household income increases, spending on energy services tends to
decrease due to adopting modern and efficient appliances (Barnes et al.,
2011), complicating the establishment of a threshold for urban and rural
areas. Karpinska and ´
Smiech (2021) try to measure the depth of energy
poverty in Poland with modelled energy cost to estimate the depth and
rate of energy poverty in case of energy transition from dirty fuels to
clean fuels. Adom et al. (2021) explored the link between development
outcomes and energy poverty (measured by energy costs and pricing)
while also considering the potential shift to green energy. The need for
price data for different energy sources, which tends to change with
global supply, makes applying the index nationally challenging.
Numerous studies have measured energy poverty by formulating a
Multidimensional Energy Poverty Index (MEPI), which focuses on energy
deprivation across multiple dimensions (Abbas et al., 2020; Bekele
et al., 2015; Gupta et al., 2020; Mendoza et al., 2019; Nussbaumer et al.,
2012; Sadath and Acharya, 2017; Santill´
an et al., 2020). It encompasses
various aspects of energy access and usage, including cooking, lighting,
the availability of household appliances, access to entertainment and
Utilities Policy 93 (2025) 101878
educational resources, and communication services. MEPI’s multidimensional
framework allows for a more comprehensive assessment of
energy poverty, recognizing that access to energy extends beyond basic
needs and plays a crucial role in overall well-being and development.
Rao et al. (2022) created an energy poverty index for the Next 11 (N11)
countries (Bangladesh, Egypt, Indonesia, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria,
Pakistan, Philippines, South Korea, Turkey, and Vietnam), considering
energy affordability, availability, and cleanability. Abbas et al. (2020)
used an adjusted multidimensional energy poverty index comprising six
indices for country-wise analysis. Sadath and Acharya (2017) created an
energy poverty index by incorporating three main dimensions: lighting,
cooking, and supplementary measures, each carrying an equivalent
weight of 33.33%. Thermal comfort and space heating are also included
in the dimensions for the creation of MEPI (Cedano et al., 2021; Mendoza
et al., 2019; Qurat-ul-Ann and Mirza, 2021; Santill´
an et al., 2020).
Though studies include various dimensions for creating an energy
poverty index using MEPI, the binary approach of MEPI makes it an
access-based matrix. Also, the subjective method to assign weights for
different dimensions is sometimes arbitrary.
The Poor People’s Energy Outlook 2010; Practical Action (2010)
introduces an energy access index based on six essential energy services
and a hybrid set of indicators quantifying qualitative aspects in household
fuels, electricity, and mechanical power supply dimensions. The
energy poverty index is primarily associated with the affordability and
accessibility of energy services. The limitation of this method is that the
relative importance assigned to different indices and the specific
threshold values are often arbitrarily determined by the authors or researchers.
This subjectivity can introduce uncertainty and potential bias
in assessing energy poverty, as these parameters may vary from one
study to another.
Similarly, the Multi-tier Energy Access Tracking Framework (MTF)
(Angelou and Bhatia, 2015) defines access to electricity and modern
energy cooking services based on adequacy, reliability, affordability,
and safety. However, MTF relies on subjective criteria for tier thresholds,
and there are challenges in accurately converting the qualitative
ranking of different tiers into quantitative measures of energy access.
Gupta et al. (2020) tried to capture the diverse aspects of energy
poverty within households by considering fifteen indices across energy
affordability, accessibility, and availability of appliances. It used a scientific
approach (i.e., PCA) for assigning weights. However, several
limitations in the study need further attention. First, most appliance
indicators used are binary, which might oversimplify household appliance
ownership and usage diversity. Also, it does not focus on the energy
sources used for lighting by different groups. It also lacks an examination
of the reliability of electricity connections, as key indicators such as
the frequency of low voltage days and power outages are not included.
Lastly, the study does not investigate the socioeconomic linkages between
household poverty and energy deprivation. Table 1 shows the
different indicators used in previous studies.
India’s rapid development and technological progress call for a “new
normal” in the definition of energy poverty, i.e., looking beyond the
availability and affordability with a focus on the quality and reliability
of energy services used by each household in India. Current approaches,
such as the Multidimensional Energy Poverty Index, often reduce energy
poverty to a simplified or binary metric, overlooking the multidimensional
nature of energy access, reliability, and efficiency, especially in
the Indian context. The subjective biases in choosing the threshold led to
challenges establishing a uniform and comprehensive assessment of
energy poverty. India faces unique challenges, such as regional disparities,
urban-rural divides in energy access, and reliance on inefficient
appliances. For example, access to electricity may vary significantly
between states and even electrified households in rural areas may
experience unreliable or inefficient energy services, which is mostly
overlooked by the existing studies (Bekele et al., 2015; Gupta et al.,
2020; Khandker et al., 2010; Nayan Yadava and Sinha, 2019; Nussbaumer
et al., 2012; Okushima, 2016; Pachauri et al., 2004; Thomson

This study addresses these limitations by introducing a comprehensive
Residential Energy Poverty Index (REPI) that not only includes
clean and green energy sources but also considers energy efficiency
(indicators like the adoption of energy-efficient appliances like fans,
lights and high energy-use appliances), appliance ownership (indicators
like ownership of communication devices, high energy-use appliances),
and accessibility and reliability of energy supply(indicators like low
voltage days, power outages, years of grid connection). These dimensions
provide a broader context to energy poverty and are beyond
solely measuring energy poverty by accessibility and affordability. They
are specifically chosen to reflect India’s multifaceted energy poverty.
The current study contributes to the existing literature in three significant
ways. First, it expands the scope by considering more relevant
energy efficiency indicators, clean and green energy, and energy appliances.
The study looks beyond traditional binary metrics, considering
that energy poverty is not just about access to essential energy but also
includes aspects such as quality of life, socioeconomic well-being and
environmental sustainability, which is reflected in the selection of indicators
under energy efficiency, clean and green energy and energy
appliance domain. Second, the study measures energy poverty in four
dimensions (clean and green energy, electricity access and reliability,
energy appliances and energy efficiency) and creates separate indices
using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) based on these four dimensions.
A state may perform well in one index and poorly in another,
resulting in an average score in the overall residential energy poverty
index but not revealing specific areas of deficiency. This approach of
categorizing indicators into different indices allows us to identify which
states lack particular dimensions and will help formulate state-specific
targeted policies and interventions. Third, the study identifies specific
socioeconomic factors that correlate with higher or lower levels of energy
poverty, offering valuable information to policymakers aiming to
reduce energy poverty across Indian states.
The REPI framework aligns closely with India’s commitment to
Sustainable Development Goal 7 (SDG 7) to ensure access to affordable,
reliable, sustainable, and modern energy for all. By capturing multidimensional
aspects such as energy accessibility, reliability, efficiency,
and clean energy use, REPI offers a comprehensive approach to identifying
and addressing energy poverty. It is particularly relevant in India,
where electrification has improved, but challenges such as unreliable
supply, energy-inefficient devices and fuels, urban-rural divides, and
regional disparities persist. In this context, REPI addresses critical gaps
by highlighting disparities between states and urban-rural areas,
enabling targeted policy interventions. It provides data-driven insights
into the reliability and quality of energy services, aspects often overlooked
in existing indices and promotes clean energy adoption and
efficient appliance use, aligning with India’s SDG 7 commitment.
2.1. Objectives
In line with this, the current study aims to address the following
objectives:
1. To formulate a comprehensive Residential Energy Poverty Index
(REPI) that evaluates energy poverty across Indian states by incorporating
nineteen indicators divided into four dimensions of energy
poverty: clean energy, electricity access and reliability, energy appliances,
and energy efficiency.
2. To analyse various factors contributing towards the increase in residential
energy poverty among Indian households, highlighting disparities
across states and between urban and rural areas.
By capturing the multidimensional aspects of energy poverty, this

3. Data and Methodology
The 2020 Indian Residential Energy Survey (IRES), conducted by the
Council on Energy, Environment, and Water (CEEW) (Agrawal et al.,
2021), is used in this study. The survey provides details of access to and
energy consumption in Indian households through nationally representative
sampling. The IRES 2020 encompassed 14,850 diverse urban
and rural households spanning 152 districts across India’s 21 most
populous states. The cumulative population of the surveyed states constitutes
a substantial portion, i.e. 97 per cent, of the overall Indian
population. It signifies the comprehensive nature of the survey by
encompassing the demographic characteristics, energy consumption
behaviour and energy accessibility from regions home to most of India’s
people. Data collected from these surveyed states is highly representative
of the population and offers valuable insights into the country’s
population dynamics, economic factors, and other relevant aspects
related to energy poverty that pertain to the nation.
The IRES employed a stratified multistage probability design for the
survey sampling. The IRES implemented a stratification strategy based
on urbanization rates and populations to capture the diverse characteristics
of sampled districts. The districts within each state were organized
by population strength, and strata were created with
approximately equal populations. Then, two districts were sampled from
each stratum using the probability proportional to size method. Within
each chosen district, a dual-layered stratum approach was employed: (i)
a rural stratum encompassing all rural areas based on the Census 2011
(Chandramouli, 2011) classification, and (ii) an urban stratum encompassing
all urban regions as defined by the Census 2011. Within these
districts, 12 villages and urban wards were selected, maintaining proportionality
to the district’s respective urban and rural populations. The
allocation of villages and wards for sampling in urban and rural strata
was determined by the proportion of the urban-rural population in the
district according to Census (2011). The distribution of villages/wards at
the stratum level was modified to be in multiples of two, guaranteeing a
presence of at least two urban wards, even in districts primarily characterized
as rural. In the selection process for rural and urban sectors,
the sampling frame was derived from the respective lists of villages and
urban wards in a district based on Census 2011 data. For the rural sector,
villages were initially arranged in descending order according to the
total number of households, and two sub-strata were created to achieve
a roughly equal population distribution. Within each sub-stratum, the
required number of sample villages (r/2) was selected using the probability
proportional to size with replacement (PPSWR) method, where
size refers to the total number of rural households in the village according
to Census (2011), and r is the number of villages to be sampled.
Villages with fewer than 50 households were excluded from the sampling
frame to minimize the need for replacements due to inadequate
sample size.
For the urban sector, the list of urban wards in a district from Census
(2011) served as the sampling frame. Like the rural sector, urban wards
were arranged in descending order of total household numbers, and two
sub-strata were formed with approximately equal populations. Within
each urban sub-stratum, the required number of sample wards (u/2) was
selected using the PPSWR strategy, where size represents the total
number of households in the urban ward as per Census 2011, and u is the
number of urban wards to be sampled. Wards with less than 50 households
were excluded from the sampling frame. A random sampling of
eight households was conducted in each village or ward, forming the
ultimate stage units for analysis. Across all districts, an equivalent
number of households (12 * 8 =96) were included in the sample. Among

3.1. Indicators
The study explores how various dimensions contribute to the overall
residential energy poverty index. The current study divides the indicators
into four categories: clean and green energy, electricity access
and reliability, energy appliance and energy efficiency. Each of these
dimensions is important in assessing and addressing the dynamics of
residential energy poverty.
Clean and green energy: The clean and green energy index captures
the transition from traditional biomass and fossil fuels to cleaner energy
sources, a critical aspect of reducing energy poverty and its associated
health impacts. It measures the proportion of clean and renewable energy
sources in the household energy mix. It includes indicators such as
the adoption of clean cooking fuels (e.g., LPG, electricity) (Abbas et al.,
2020; Gowon Edoumiekumo et al., 2013; Santill´
an et al., 2020), and
reliance on traditional fuels (e.g., firewood, dung cake, diesel generators)(Crentsil
et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2020; Nayan Yadava and Sinha,
2019). Reducing reliance on traditional biomass for cooking mitigates
air pollution and improves health outcomes. A higher score in this index
signifies a reduced dependence on polluting and unsustainable energy
sources, leading to enhanced environmental sustainability, improved
health outcomes, and a lower risk of energy poverty.
Electricity access and reliability: This dimension depicts the challenges
encountered by households in accessing electricity (Bonatz et al.,
2019; Cedano et al., 2021; Culver, 2017; Jayasinghe et al., 2021;
Nussbaumer et al., 2012). Regular and reliable access to electricity is a
cornerstone of modern living and is directly linked to energy poverty. It
encompasses metrics such as provision of grid supply, rate of power
reliability, duration of power outages, voltage stability, and daily hours
for availability of electricity. Grid supply availability indicates whether
households are connected to the national grid, providing a fundamental
measure of access to electricity. But despite the rapid grid connection,
the study by Aklin et al. (2016) shows that rural households faced 11 h of
electricity outages on average every day. Hence, indicators like power
cuts and low voltage days become important in revealing the reliability
and quality of that access. The number of hours electricity is available
during peak usage times (6 pm–12 am) captures the effectiveness and
reliability of the grid when demand is high, which is critical for
household activities such as cooking, studying, and using electronic
devices. The years of grid connection are important for measuring
electricity reliability because they show how long a household has been
connected to the power grid. This duration indicates whether there has
been sufficient time to improve infrastructure and services, which can
lead to better quality and stability over time. Access to electricity is
essential for lighting, heating, cooling, and powering appliances. It
supports economic activities, education, and healthcare and significantly
enhances the overall quality of life. A higher score in this index
denotes improved access to electricity, which is essential for fulfilling
basic energy requirements, enhancing the quality of life, and mitigating
the risk of energy poverty.
Energy appliances: Appliances are critical in converting available
energy into functional end-use services. This category groups indicators
based on the ownership and usage of various household appliances,
which indicate a household’s energy consumption pattern and standard
of living. Access to energy appliances is a crucial dimension as it reflects
the availability and use of modern energy technologies that can enhance
productivity, improve living conditions, and contribute to
Utilities Policy 93 (2025) 101878
socioeconomic development (Adusah-Poku and Takeuchi, 2019; Crentsil
et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2020; Jayasinghe et al., 2021; Nussbaumer
et al., 2012; Santill´
an et al., 2020). Fewer fans and lights indicate
insufficient access to cooling, lighting, and thermal comfort, affecting
quality of life. Similarly, communication devices per household member
reflect economic or infrastructural limitations, hindering convenience
and digital inclusion. Ownership of appliances like refrigerators and
washing machines indicates improved living standards in terms of
convenience, hygiene, and food preservation. A higher score within this
index indicates improved access to energy appliances that contribute to
elevated living standards, increased productivity, and enhanced comfort,
diminishing the prevalence of energy poverty.
Energy efficiency: The vulnerability of households to energy poverty
is significantly compounded by energy inefficiency, mainly attributable
to substantial energy losses occurring within households (Li et al., 2021;
Papada and Kaliampakos, 2019; Zhao et al., 2022). This category focuses
on the efficiency of energy consumption within households. Adopting
energy-efficient appliances like fans, lights, and high energy-use appliances
helps reduce energy consumption and lower household energy
bills, leading to significant cost savings and increasing energy affordability.
Improved energy efficiency also ensures better resource management.
High values within this index signify a greater integration of
energy-efficient appliances, leading to decreased energy consumption
and lower household utility bills. It reflects the degree of investment in
energy-efficient technologies and the capacity for energy conservation,
thereby contributing to endeavours aimed at alleviating energy poverty.
The interaction of these dimensions is complex, and an overall
assessment is required to estimate residential energy poverty accurately.
This multidimensional structure provides a comprehensive base for
addressing the complexities of energy poverty. All the indicators are
discussed further in detail in Table 2.
3.2. Analytical model
3.2.1. Principal component analysis (PCA)
The next step involves constructing indexes using principal component
analysis (PCA). Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a widely
used statistical technique applied in various fields (consumer behaviour
analysis, climate data analysis, and image and signal processing),
including indexing. PCA is a mathematical approach to identifying and
summarizing patterns within a dataset by reducing its dimensionality.
PCA offers a valuable tool for capturing essential information and
generating composite indices that can simplify complex datasets.
Normalising the indicators is the first step in forming the residential
energy poverty index. Normalization is done to bring all the indicators to
the same scale (0–1), where the household with the lowest value of the
selected energy poverty indicator gets a value of 0, whereas a value of 1
is assigned to the one with the highest value of the particular energy
poverty indicator. Also, it helps to make all the indicators positively
related to residential energy poverty.

3.2.2. Multiple linear regression
After creating the REPI, multiple linear regression (MLR) depicts the
relationship between the residential energy poverty index and various
socioeconomic variables (Darlington and Hayes, 2016). This method
explains the relationship between the dependent variable (REPI) and
explanatory variables (area of residence, gender, education level, type of
house, caste, income source and monthly expenditure) using a linear
combination of the explanatory variables. Explanatory variables,
including area of residence, gender, education level, type of house,
caste, income source, and monthly expenditure, are derived from
existing literature, emphasizing their relevance in understanding energy
poverty dynamics. Abbas et al. (2020) and Sharma et al. (2019) highlight
the importance of socio-demographic factors like area of residence
and housing type in determining energy vulnerability. Gender and education
level have been linked to differential access to resources and
energy use patterns. Income source and monthly expenditure are critical
economic indicators directly affecting energy affordability and consumption
(Sharma et al., 2019). Caste, a socio-cultural factor particularly
relevant in the Indian context, has been shown to influence energy

4.2. Dimensions of energy poverty
4.2.1. Clean and green energy
Clean energy sources play an important role in the broader context of
residential energy poverty. They align closely with the Sustainable
Development Goals, particularly SDGs 3 and 7 (United Nations, 2023).
Using non-clean energy sources has detrimental effects on the environment
and individual health. Fig. 6 illustrates the overall clean and green
energy situations across Indian states. Delhi, Telangana, and Maharashtra
scored highest in the clean energy index, whereas states like
Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand and Madhya Pradesh scored least in the clean
and green energy index. As per the Ministry of Petroleum and Natural
Gas, Delhi, Telangana and Maharashtra constitute 477.3 lakhs of active
domestic customers of LPG, whereas Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand and
Madhya Pradesh constitute only 288.4 lakhs of active domestic LPG
customers (MoP&NG, 2023). The government initiated the Ujjwala
scheme to promote the use of clean energy for cooking, facilitating the
addition of approximately 17 crore LPG consumers over the past nine
years.
Furthermore, to discourage kerosene use for cooking, the government
eliminated the kerosene subsidy in 2021(Rao, 2012). In states with
lower electricity accessibility and reliability, there is a greater reliance
on non-clean energy sources for heating, lighting, and cooking. This
correlation is highlighted by examining the relationship between
accessibility, reliability and clean energy. Expanding the reach of the
Unnat Chulha Abhiyan (UCA), launched in 2014 to promote improved
biomass cookstoves, is important for facilitating the transition from
traditional fuels to cleaner alternatives for cooking. The National Efficient
Cooking Program (NECP), introduced in 2023, offers
induction-based cookstoves with a 25–30% cost advantage over traditional
cooking methods, promising significant energy savings and
cost-effective cooking solutions for rural India (PIB, 2023c).
4.2.2. Electricity access and reliability
Fig. 7 presents a detailed overview of overall electricity access and
reliability. The dimension encompasses factors such as grid supply
provision, occurrences of low voltage, instances of power cuts within the
existing power connection, years of grid connection and hours of electricity
available in the evening. This dimension is pivotal in mitigating
energy poverty while fostering sustainable and inclusive growth. Certain
southern states, notably Tamil Nadu and Kerala, Gujarat, and Maharashtra
exhibit better energy quality performance than other Indian states.
Contrastingly, Uttar Pradesh, Assam, Bihar, Jharkhand, and Chhattisgarh
exhibit the least electricity access and reliability levels among all
the states under study. This result is consistent for both rural and urban
areas. This results in their heavy reliance on traditional agro-based fuels
for their cooking, lighting, and heating needs. Inadequate electricity
access and reliability affect households’ use of non-clean energy sources,
impacting human health and environmental well-being. The government’s
efforts under the SAUBHAGYA (Pradhan Mantri Sahaj Bijli Har
Ghar Yojana) scheme, which targets last-mile electrification and reports
100 per cent electrification in recent years, should now focus on
ensuring a stable and consistent electricity supply to every household.
Strengthening programs like the Deen Dayal Upadhyaya Gram Jyoti

4.2.4. Energy efficiency
Fig. 10 provides an illustrative view of the overall energy efficiency
dimension within residential energy poverty. States such as Delhi,
Odisha, Himachal Pradesh, and West Bengal have higher energy efficiency
index scores than other states under study. On the other hand,
states like Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu
continue to face significant energy inefficiencies, worsening the challenges
of reducing energy poverty for their residents. Energy inefficiency
significantly increases household vulnerability to energy poverty, primarily
due to substantial energy losses at the household level. Inefficient
appliances contribute to excessive energy consumption, driving utility
costs and placing additional financial strain on economically disadvantaged
households. These inefficiencies hinder access to affordable and
reliable energy and perpetuate a cycle of poverty, as limited resources
are diverted towards high energy bills instead of essential needs such as
food, education, and healthcare. The Bureau of Energy Efficiency’s
(BEE) star labelling program to promote energy-efficient appliances is a
major step. The Energy Efficient Fans Programme (EEFP), launched in
2023 and promised to deliver one crore energy-efficient fans in the
initial phase, will help increase the energy-efficient appliance penetration
in Indian households (PIB, 2023c). The effectiveness of energy efficiency
measures is closely tied to the quality of the energy supply. In
regions where the supply is unreliable or of low quality, the benefits of
energy-efficient practices and technologies may not be fully realized. As
discussed in the electricity supply and reliability section, this barrier
needs to be addressed by working on infrastructure improvements.
Addressing these inefficiencies through targeted interventions and
capacity-building initiatives is essential to alleviating energy poverty
and promoting inclusive economic development nationwide.

4.3. REPI for urban and rural India
States with high energy poverty in rural areas might not necessarily
exhibit similar dynamics within urban contexts. This divergence can be
attributed to the uneven distribution of wealth and distinct trajectories
of urbanization experienced by states. The contrasting patterns
emphasize the importance of adapting energy poverty alleviation policies
to account for the unique challenges of urban and rural environments.
In India, there is a wide variation in energy consumption patterns
across rural and urban regions. In rural regions, the use of firewood,
traditional cookstoves and biomass burning dominate the energy
choices of households, while the use of LPG, electric induction, AC, and
refrigerators showcase the energy consumption pattern of urban
households (Acharya and Sadath, 2019; Sadath and Acharya, 2017). It
thus becomes essential to understand how energy poverty varies across
rural and urban households in India and what specific interventions can
be used to reduce energy poverty.
Fig. 11a shows a clear differentiation in residential energy poverty
between rural and urban settings within each state. The consistent trend
of higher energy poverty in rural areas holds across all states. On
average, India’s rural energy poverty index (REPI) stands at 0.622, while
urban REPI is notably lower at 0.386. This significant disparity is
evident throughout all Indian states. The disparity between urban and
rural regions is attributed to multiple factors, including the poor quality
of energy services, power disruption and lack of awareness about
energy-efficient appliances. Our study vividly depicts the substantial
contrast between rural and urban India, which aligns with the conclusions
of previous studies (Acharya and Sadath, 2019; Gupta et al., 2020;
Sadath and Acharya, 2017). Chhattisgarh scored high energy poverty in
urban areas, whereas Madhya Pradesh scored high in rural areas. This
finding can be attributed to the persistence of traditional fuel usage in
rural regions despite LPG connections. LPG usage is limited by infrastructure
challenges, which include issues related to transportation,
road connectivity, and the availability of LPG distribution centres, as
well as limited financial resources for LPG refills and a lack of awareness
regarding the benefits of LPG.
Urban-Rural differences in REPI components:
•Clean and green energy: Urban households have better access to
clean energy sources like LPG and electricity. In contrast, rural
households continue to rely heavily on biomass, with clean energy
access being limited by logistical and economic barriers. States like
Bihar and West Bengal highlight this disparity (Fig. 11b), with the
highest difference between urban and rural scores on clean and green
energy access.
•Electricity access and reliability: While electrification rates have
improved nationally, rural areas struggle with unreliable power
supply and frequent disruptions. Fig. 11c scores show that urban
areas generally score higher in access and reliability. States like
Assam and Bihar have the lowest scores in rural electricity access and
reliability, while states like Himachal Pradesh and Kerala score
higher in rural electricity access and reliability.
•Energy appliance: Urban areas display significantly higher penetration
of energy-efficient and modern appliances than rural areas
(Fig. 11d). States like Delhi, Assam and Punjab lead in urban appliance
ownership, reflecting their advanced infrastructure and higher
income levels. In contrast, rural areas in states such as Bihar, Madhya
Pradesh, and Jharkhand lag far behind, reflecting limited affordability
and a lack of awareness of modern energy technologies.
•Energy efficiency: Urban areas benefit from better awareness and
access to energy-efficient appliances, reflected in higher scores in
states like Delhi and Kerala (Fig. 11e). In rural areas, however, energy
efficiency is rarely a priority, with affordability and immediate
functionality taking precedence (Sankhyayan and Dasgupta, 2019).

importance of economic development in addressing energy poverty, as
higher income levels generally translate to better access to energy resources,
infrastructure, and appliances.
5. Policy recommendations
Based on the study’s findings, a three-tier government-level
approach is proposed, involving the central government, state government,
and local government.
National level: The analysis identified Chhattisgarh, Bihar, Madhya
Pradesh, and Jharkhand as having the highest levels of residential energy
poverty. These states also perform poorly in individual dimensions
of clean and green energy, electricity access, and reliability. Therefore,
implementing a National Clean Energy Policy (NCEP) that aligns with
the National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC) is recommended.
This policy should focus on regional variations and allocate a higher
budget for energy poverty alleviation programs exclusively targeting
these states.
State level: The results show that states like Chhattisgarh, Bihar, and
Jharkhand, which struggle with residential energy poverty, should focus
on rural electrification and promoting clean cooking fuel, integrating
schemes like SAUBHAGYA and DDUGJY. Additionally, states such as
Assam and Uttar Pradesh, which perform poorly in electricity access and
reliability index, can adopt a decentralized solar home system to
improve electricity availability. Therefore, developing and implementing
state-specific Energy Poverty Action Plans (EPAP) is crucial at the
state level. Offering subsidies for clean energy adoption can also help
alleviate energy poverty. States with lower clean and green energy
indices can learn from successful models implemented in Delhi, Telangana,
and Karnataka’s Renewable Energy Policy 2022–2027
Utilities Policy 93 (2025) 101878
(Government of Karnataka, 2022), which provides subsidies and incentives
to households and small industries for setting up solar projects.
Local level (Village, block, community level): The findings show high
residential energy poverty in rural areas across all the states. So, at the
local level, the focus should be on community-based renewable energy
projects, in which local government and leaders should be involved to
ensure that solutions are specific to community needs. It will foster
participatory decision-making, which can, in turn, enhance the sustainability
of the energy projects. Implementing decentralized solar
power in energy-poor areas can improve energy access and reliability.
Programs like PM-KUSUM can promote solar power among farmers, and
combining these with subsidized installations can boost energy
affordability.
Focus on energy efficiency: According to the findings, Madhya Pradesh,
Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh are performing poorly in the energy
appliance and efficiency indexes. State governments thus have significant
opportunities to implement demand-side management policies.
Encouraging the use of energy-efficient appliances and technologies can
reduce the financial burden on residents, stimulate market transformation,
and foster innovation. Energy efficiency initiatives, such as
the Energy Efficient Fans Programme (EEFP) and National Efficient
Cooking Programme (NECP) launched in 2023, can help households
reduce overall energy consumption and lower energy bills.
Focus on energy cleanliness, quality and reliability: Clean energy initiatives
should promote clean cooking fuels in the rural areas of Jharkhand,
Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, and Odisha, which have the lowest
residential energy poverty scores. This shift from traditional fuels to
clean energy in cooking and lighting will help improve their health to a
significant extent. Expanding the reach of the Unnat Chulha Abhiyan
(UCA), launched in 2014 and increasing LPG refill subsidy under
Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana (PMUY) will ensure reliable energy
supply in both urban and rural areas. Respective state governments
should rationalize energy subsidies to target clean energy resources
more efficiently, redirecting them from traditional fossil fuels to greener
sources like off-grid renewable energy.
Awareness campaigns through energy literacy: Energy literacy is
particularly relevant in our analysis, as gender and literacy levels show a
significant positive association with energy poverty. Promoting energy
literacy, including financial energy literacy, action energy literacy, and
device energy literacy, as suggested by van den Broek (2019), through
educational campaigns on energy tariffs, demand response programs,
and renewable energy is critical in urban and rural settings. These
campaigns can enable residents to make informed choices about their
energy use, potentially reducing energy bills and lowering their environmental
footprint.
Focus on gender-inclusive policies: As per the findings, households with
female heads negatively correlate with energy poverty, making women a
key stakeholder in reducing residential energy poverty. Women supported
through self-help groups (SHGs) can be pivotal in promoting
clean energy practices in rural areas, especially in cooking practices. The
central government’s distribution of solar cookers can significantly
enhance the delivery of affordable, clean and green energy for cooking
to many villages. Similar targeted subsidy programs for marginal groups
can also encourage a comprehensive and inclusive approach to energy
poverty alleviation.
Each region has unique challenges and needs, and a differentiated
and targeted strategy is indeed the need of the hour. Recognizing the
impact of multiple factors on energy poverty allows policymakers to
develop strategies that can address energy poverty comprehensively,
resulting in more effective and sustainable solutions.
6. Conclusion
The study demonstrates that residential energy poverty is not
confined to a single dimension but presents itself as a complex and
multidimensional challenge. The findings of the study state that
