### **ABSTRACT (250 words)**

**Background:** Water infrastructure development, particularly piped water systems, is considered essential for achieving SDG 6. However, infrastructure without reliability may create new vulnerabilities.

**Objective:** This study examines the paradoxical relationship between improved water infrastructure and water disruption using nationally representative data from India.

**Methods:** We analyzed 636,699 households from NFHS-5 (2019-21), comparing water disruption rates across different water sources and developing a novel Infrastructure Dependency Index.

**Results:** Counter-intuitively, households with piped water reported 52.8% disruption rates compared to 27.3% for tube well users and 23.5% for protected well users. Urban areas showed higher disruption (48.3%) than rural areas (26.2%). Wealthy households with piped infrastructure experienced more disruption than poor households with traditional sources. Multivariate analysis confirmed that piped water increases disruption odds by 3.25 times (95% CI: 3.14-3.36) after controlling for socioeconomic factors.

**Conclusions:** Infrastructure development without ensuring reliability creates new forms of vulnerability. Households dependent on centralized systems lose traditional water management knowledge and face greater disruption when systems fail. Policy should focus on reliability, redundancy, and maintaining diverse water sources rather than solely expanding piped coverage.

### **5. DISCUSSION (4-5 pages)**

#### **5.1 Understanding the Paradox**

**Why Piped Water Increases Disruption**

*Supply-side factors:*
"Piped systems require continuous electricity for pumping, regular maintenance, and complex distribution networks. In India, these systems face frequent breakdowns due to power outages, pipe bursts, and inadequate maintenance budgets..."

*Demand-side factors:*
"Piped water creates dependency and expectation. Households abandon traditional sources, lose water storage practices, and expect continuous supply. When disruption occurs, they lack alternatives..."

*System complexity:*
"Centralized systems have multiple failure points. A single pump failure or pipe burst affects thousands of households, while well failure affects only immediate users..."

#### **5.2 Reconceptualizing Water Vulnerability**

**Traditional Vulnerability Model:**
```
Poverty → No Infrastructure → Poor Water Access → Bad Health Outcomes
```

**Infrastructure Dependency Model (Our Contribution):**
```
Infrastructure Without Reliability → Dependency → Lost Alternatives → Greater Disruption
```

"This new framework explains why wealthy urban households with piped water may be more water insecure than poor rural households with reliable wells..."

#### **5.3 Coping Mechanisms and Adaptive Capacity**

**Two Distinct Coping Paradigms:**

*Traditional Source Users:*
- Labor-intensive strategies (walking farther, waiting longer)
- Community cooperation (sharing sources)
- Traditional knowledge (knowing multiple sources)
- Time costs but not monetary costs

*Infrastructure Dependent Users:*
- Market-based solutions (buying water)
- Remaining within infrastructure system (tankers)
- Individual rather than community responses  
- Monetary costs replace time costs

"These different coping strategies have equity implications. Poor households with piped water must purchase alternatives during disruptions, while wealthy households with wells simply walk to their backup source..."

#### **5.4 Policy Implications**

**Rethinking Infrastructure Development**

"Our findings don't argue against piped water but highlight that infrastructure without reliability may worsen water security. Policy implications include:"

1. **Reliability Over Coverage**: Prioritize making existing systems reliable before expanding
2. **Maintain Redundancy**: Preserve traditional sources as backups
3. **Storage Infrastructure**: Household and community storage to buffer disruptions
4. **Differential Support**: Recognize that modern infrastructure creates new vulnerabilities requiring different interventions

**The Jal Jeevan Mission Paradox**

"India's push for universal piped water may inadvertently increase water insecurity if reliability isn't ensured. The program should incorporate reliability metrics, backup sources, and storage capacity..."

### **6. LIMITATIONS**

"Several limitations merit consideration:

1. **Cross-sectional Design**: We cannot establish causation, only association
2. **Disruption Measure**: Binary variable doesn't capture frequency or duration
3. **Self-reported Data**: Potential recall and social desirability bias
4. **Temporal Mismatch**: Infrastructure recent, disruption past 2 weeks
5. **Unobserved Heterogeneity**: Water quality, pressure, timing not measured"

---

### **7. CONCLUSION**

"This study documents a troubling paradox in water development: households with modern piped infrastructure experience more water disruption than those with traditional sources. Using NFHS-5 data from 636,699 households, we find piped water increases disruption odds by 3.25 times, with effects strongest for wealthy urban households.

We propose a new framework - infrastructure dependency vulnerability - to explain how modernization without reliability creates new forms of water insecurity. Households become locked into unreliable systems, lose traditional alternatives, and face greater hardship when systems fail.

These findings have profound implications for water policy. The global push for universal piped water, exemplified by India's Jal Jeevan Mission, may inadvertently decrease water security if reliability isn't prioritized. Development should focus not just on infrastructure expansion but on reliability, redundancy, and resilience.

Future research should explore temporal dynamics of disruption, examine water quality alongside quantity, and test interventions that balance modern infrastructure with traditional resilience. As climate change increases water stress, understanding and addressing infrastructure dependency becomes critical for achieving true water security."
