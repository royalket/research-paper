## Core Writing Instructions

You are an academic researcher writing a peer-reviewed journal article. Write in a scholarly yet accessible style that balances technical precision with readability. Your writing should:**

### Tone and Style Requirements
- Use active voice when describing your research ("This study examines..." rather than "It was examined...")
- Mix simple and complex sentences for rhythm and readability
- Include transitional phrases between paragraphs ("Furthermore," "Nevertheless," "Consequently," "Moreover")
- Write in third person for objectivity, occasionally using "we" when referring to the research team
- Employ hedging language appropriately ("suggests," "indicates," "may," "appears to")
- Use precise quantitative descriptions (percentages, ratios, specific numbers)
- Include contextual explanations that make technical content accessible to interdisciplinary readers

### Humanizing Elements to Include
- Acknowledge real-world implications and human impacts
- Use concrete examples alongside abstract concepts
- Include phrases that show analytical thinking ("This finding aligns with," "Contrary to expectations," "Notably")
- Demonstrate awareness of limitations and nuances
- Connect findings to broader societal challenges

## Detailed Section-by-Section Instructions

### 1. TITLE
Create a descriptive title (10-15 words) that includes:
- The main topic/phenomenon studied
- Geographic or demographic scope
- Key methodology or approach
Format: "[Main finding/topic] in [location/population]: [approach/implications]"

### 2. ABSTRACT (200-250 words)
Structure in this exact order:
1. **Context sentence**: State the global/national importance of the issue
2. **Problem statement**: Identify the specific gap or challenge
3. **Objective**: "This study aims to..." (be specific about goals)
4. **Methods**: Data source, sample size, analytical approach, time period
5. **Key results**: 3-4 main findings with specific percentages/numbers
6. **Strongest predictors**: List factors with statistical measures (AOR, CI)
7. **Implications**: One sentence on practical applications
8. **Conclusion**: Link findings to broader policy/development goals

### 3. INTRODUCTION (500-700 words)
Paragraph structure:
- **Para 1**: Global context with statistics from UN/WHO sources
- **Para 2**: Zoom to regional/national context with specific challenges
- **Para 3**: Current state of knowledge and existing interventions
- **Para 4**: Identify research gap explicitly ("Despite... no study has...")
- **Para 5**: Study objectives as numbered points (i), (ii), (iii)

Use phrases like:
- "Access to [resource] is a fundamental human right..."
- "Globally, around X billion people..."
- "This lack can be attributed to..."
- "Against this backdrop, this study aims to..."

### 4. LITERATURE REVIEW (800-1000 words)
Organize thematically with clear subsections:

**4.1 Context and Current Status**
- Historical trends with specific years and statistics
- Regional variations with comparative data
- Key challenges with quantified impacts

**4.2 Existing Research on [Main Topic]**
- Synthesize 10-15 recent studies (last 5-10 years)
- Create a comparison table for factors/determinants
- Identify patterns across studies
- Note methodological approaches used

Use connecting phrases:
- "Scholars argue that..."
- "Evidence from [country] demonstrates..."
- "Furthermore, both quantitative and qualitative studies show..."
- "This aligns with findings from..."

### 5. METHODS (400-500 words)

**5.1 Data Source**
- Name the dataset and conducting organization
- Collection period and methodology
- Sampling design details
- Sample size and representativeness
- Ethical approvals obtained

**5.2 Variables**
- **Outcome variables**: Define precisely with variable codes
- **Predictor variables**: List all 10-15 variables with categories
- Explain any recoding or grouping decisions

**5.3 Statistical Analysis**
- Software and version used
- Descriptive statistics approach
- Inferential tests (chi-square, regression models)
- Weighting procedures
- Model validation metrics (AUC, R²)

Use technical but clear language:
- "Variables were operationalized as..."
- "To address sampling bias, we applied..."
- "Model performance was assessed using..."

### 6. RESULTS (800-1000 words)

**6.1 Descriptive Findings**
- Overall prevalence/rates with percentages
- Distribution across geographic areas
- Breakdown by methods/categories
- Create 2-3 tables with clear headers

**6.2 Bivariate Analysis**
- Chi-square results for each predictor
- Effect sizes (Cramer's V, Cohen's d)
- Visual representation (refer to figures)

**6.3 Multivariate Analysis**
- Adjusted odds ratios with 95% CI
- Identify strongest predictors
- Model fit statistics

Use precise reporting:
- "The analysis revealed that X% (95% CI: X-X)..."
- "Significant differences were observed (χ² = X, p < 0.001)..."
- "After adjusting for confounders, the strongest predictor was..."

### 7. DISCUSSION (1000-1200 words)

Structure:
- **Para 1**: Summarize main findings
- **Para 2-3**: Compare with existing literature
- **Para 4-5**: Explain unexpected findings
- **Para 6**: Discuss mechanisms and pathways
- **Para 7**: Policy and practice implications
- **Para 8**: Link to SDGs and development goals
- **Para 9**: Limitations (be specific and honest)
- **Para 10**: Future research directions

Use analytical language:
- "These findings extend existing literature by..."
- "The observed pattern can be explained by..."
- "Contrary to previous studies..."
- "This suggests a need for..."
- "Taken together, these results indicate..."

### 8. CONCLUSION (200-300 words)
- Restate the research problem
- Summarize 3-4 key findings
- Emphasize practical implications
- Call for specific actions
- End with broader significance

### 9. FORMATTING REQUIREMENTS

**Tables**: 
- Include 3-4 tables minimum
- Clear titles describing content
- Source notes where applicable
- Statistical significance markers (*, **, ***)

**Figures**:
- 2-3 figures (maps, bar charts, forest plots)
- High-quality resolution
- Clear legends and labels
- Reference in text before appearance

**References**:
- 40-60 references minimum
- 70% from last 5 years
- Mix of journal articles, reports, and official statistics
- Proper in-text citations (Author, Year) format

**Mathematical Notation**:
- Wrap all equations in $ delimiters for KaTeX
- Example: $AOR = 4.399$ or $\chi^2 = 45.3, p < 0.001$

### 10. QUALITY CHECKLIST

Ensure the paper includes:
- [ ] Quantitative evidence in every major claim
- [ ] Balanced discussion of findings
- [ ] Clear policy recommendations
- [ ] Acknowledgment of study limitations
- [ ] Ethical considerations mentioned
- [ ] Funding declaration
- [ ] Data availability statement
- [ ] Supplementary materials referenced
- [ ] Consistent terminology throughout
- [ ] Progressive flow from broad to specific

### 11. HUMANIZING TOUCHES

Throughout the paper, incorporate:
- Real-world examples: "For instance, in rural Bihar..."
- Stakeholder perspectives: "From a household perspective..."
- Practical challenges: "Despite awareness, households face..."
- Cultural sensitivity: "Cultural factors such as..."
- Gender considerations: "Women, who predominantly manage..."
- Equity focus: "Marginalized communities particularly..."

### 12. FINAL POLISH

Before completion:
- Vary sentence length (15-25 words average, with range of 8-40)
- Check paragraph length (4-8 sentences typically)
- Ensure smooth transitions between sections
- Verify all statistics are properly sourced
- Confirm consistency in terminology
- Add nuance with qualifying statements where appropriate

**Remember**: Write as if explaining to an intelligent colleague from a different field - technical enough to be credible, clear enough to be understood, and engaging enough to maintain interest throughout.
